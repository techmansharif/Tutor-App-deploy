\section*{Introduction to Exponents and Logarithms}

Very large or very small numbers or expressions can easily be expressed by using exponents. As a result, calculations and solution of mathematical problems become easier. Scientific or standard form of a number is expressed by using exponents. Therefore, every student should have the knowledge of exponents and its applications.

Exponents beget logarithms. Multiplication and division of numbers or expressions and exponent-related calculations have become easier with the help of logarithms. Use of logarithm in scientific calculations was the only way before calculators and computers became available. Still, the use of logarithm is important as an alternative to calculators and computers.

In this chapter, exponents and logarithms have been discussed in detail. At the end of the chapter, the students will be able to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Explain the rational exponent.
    \item Explain and apply the positive integral exponents, zero, and negative integral exponents.
    \item Solve problems by describing and applying the rules of exponents.
    \item Explain the $n$th root and rational fractional exponents and express the $n$th root in terms of exponents.
    \item Explain logarithms.
    \item Prove and apply the formulae of logarithms.
    \item Explain natural logarithm and common logarithm.
    \item Explain the scientific form of numbers.
    \item Explain the characteristic and mantissa of common logarithm.
    \item Calculate common and natural logarithm by calculator.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Exponents or Indices}

In class VI, we got the idea of exponents, and in class VII, we learned the exponential rules for multiplication and division. Expressions associated with exponent and base are called exponential expressions.

\hline
Successive multiplication of the same expression & Exponential expression & base & power or exponent \\
\hline
$2 \times 2 \times 2$ & $2^3$ & 2 & 3 \\
$3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3$ & $3^4$ & 3 & 4 \\
$a \times a \times a$ & $a^3$ & $a$ & 3 \\
$b \times b \times b \times b \times b$ & $b^5$ & $b$ & 5 \\

If $a$ is any real number, successive multiplication of $n$ $a$s is $a^n$; that is, $a \times a \times a \times \ldots \times a$ ($n$ times $a$) = $a^n$, where $n$ is a positive integer. Here, $n$ is the index or power, and $a$ is the base. Conversely, $a^n = a \times a \times a \times \ldots \times a$ ($n$ times $a$).

Exponents may not only be positive integers; they may be negative integers or positive or negative fractions. That is, for base $a \in \mathbb{R}$ (set of real numbers) and power $n \in \mathbb{Q}$ (set of rational numbers), $a^n$ is defined. The case of $n \in \mathbb{N}$ (set of natural numbers) is especially considered. Besides, exponents may also be irrational, but as it is out of the curriculum, it has not been discussed in this chapter.

\subsection*{Index Laws}

Let $a \in \mathbb{R}$ (set of real numbers) and $m, n \in \mathbb{N}$ (set of natural numbers).

\textbf{Formula 1 (Multiplication):} $a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$

\textbf{Formula 2 (Division):} 
\[
\frac{a^m}{a^n} = \begin{cases} 
a^{m-n} & \text{when } m \geq n \\ 
\frac{1}{a^{n-m}} & \text{when } n > m 
\end{cases}
\]

Fill in the blanks of the following table:

\begin{tabular}{|p{5cm}|p{3cm}|p{5cm}|p{3cm}|}
\hline
$a \neq 0, m > n$ & $m=5, n=3$ & $a \neq 0, n > m$ & $m=3, n=5$ \\
\hline
$a^5 \times a^3 = (a \times a \times a \times a \times a) \times (a \times a \times a)$ & $a^8 = a^{5+3}$ & $a^3 \times a^5 = a^8$ & $a^8$ \\
$= a \times a \times a \times a \times a \times a \times a \times a = a^8 = a^{5+3}$ & & & \\
$\frac{a^5}{a^3} = a^{5-3} = a^2$ & $a^2$ & $\frac{a^3}{a^5} = \frac{1}{a^{5-3}} = \frac{1}{a^2}$ & $\frac{1}{a^2}$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}

Therefore, in general, $a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n}$ and 
\[
\frac{a^m}{a^n} = \begin{cases} 
a^{m-n} & \text{when } m \geq n \\ 
\frac{1}{a^{n-m}} & \text{when } n > m 
\end{cases}
\]

\textbf{Formula 3 (Power of Product):} $(ab)^n = a^n \times b^n$

We observe:
\[
(5 \times 2)^3 = (5 \times 2) \times (5 \times 2) \times (5 \times 2) = (5 \times 5 \times 5) \times (2 \times 2 \times 2) = 5^3 \times 2^3
\]
In general:
\[
(ab)^n = ab \times ab \times ab \times \ldots \times ab \quad [\text{successive multiplication of } n \, ab\text{'s}]
\]
\[
= (a \times a \times a \times \ldots \times a) \times (b \times b \times b \times \ldots \times b) = a^n \times b^n
\]

\textbf{Formula 4 (Power of Quotient):} $\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}, (b \neq 0)$

We observe:
\[
\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^3 = \frac{5}{2} \times \frac{5}{2} \times \frac{5}{2} = \frac{5^3}{2^3}
\]
In general:
\[
\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{a}{b} \times \ldots \times \frac{a}{b} \quad [\text{successive multiplication of } n \text{ times } \frac{a}{b}]
\]
\[
= \frac{a \times a \times a \times \ldots \times a}{b \times b \times b \times \ldots \times b} = \frac{a^n}{b^n}
\]

\textbf{Formula 5 (Power of Power):} $\left(a^m\right)^n = a^{mn}$

\[
\left(a^m\right)^n = a^m \times a^m \times a^m \times \ldots \times a^m \quad [\text{successive multiplication of } n \text{ times } a^m]
\]
\[
= a^{m+m+m+\ldots+m} \quad [\text{in the power, multiplication of } n \text{ times of exponent}]
\]
\[
= a^{m \times n} = a^{mn}
\]
Therefore, $\left(a^m\right)^n = a^{mn}$.

\part{Zero and Negative Indices}
% Defining the section for Zero and Negative Indices
\section*{Zero and Negative Indices}

For zero and negative indices, $a^0$ and $a^{-n}$ are defined as follows (where $n$ is a natural number).

\textbf{Definition 1 (Zero Index):} $a^0 = 1, (a \neq 0)$

\textbf{Definition 2 (Negative Index):} $a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}, (a \neq 0, n \in \mathbb{N})$

For all integer indices $m$ and $n$, the definition $\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}$ holds true.

Observe:
\[
\frac{a^n}{a^n} = a^{n-n} = a^0
\]
But:
\[
\frac{a^n}{a^n} = \frac{a \times a \times a \times \ldots \times a \quad (n \text{ times})}{a \times a \times a \times \ldots \times a \quad (n \text{ times})} = 1
\]
Therefore, $a^0 = 1$.

And:
\[
\frac{1}{a^n} = \frac{a^0}{a^n} = a^{0-n} = a^{-n}
\]

\textbf{Example 1.} Find the values:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\frac{5^2}{5^3}$
    \item $\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^5 \times \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{-5}$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\frac{5^2}{5^3} = 5^{2-3} = 5^{-1} = \frac{1}{5^1} = \frac{1}{5}$
    \item $\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^5 \times \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{-5} = \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{5-5} = \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^0 = 1$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Example 2.} Simplify:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\frac{5^4 \times 8 \times 16}{2^5 \times 125}$
    \item $\frac{3 \cdot 2^n - 4 \cdot 2^{n-2}}{2^n - 2^{n-1}}$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\frac{5^4 \times 8 \times 16}{2^5 \times 125} = \frac{5^4 \times 2^3 \times 2^4}{2^5 \times 5^3} = \frac{5^4 \times 2^{3+4}}{5^3 \times 2^5} = \frac{5^4}{5^3} \times \frac{2^7}{2^5} = 5^{4-3} \times 2^{7-5} = 5^1 \times 2^2 = 5 \times 4 = 20$
    \item $\frac{3 \cdot 2^n - 4 \cdot 2^{n-2}}{2^n - 2^{n-1}} = \frac{3 \cdot 2^n - 2^2 \cdot 2^{n-2}}{2^n - 2^n \cdot 2^{-1}} = \frac{3 \cdot 2^n - 2^{2+n-2}}{2^n - 2^n \cdot \frac{1}{2}} = \frac{3 \cdot 2^n - 2^n}{\left(1 - \frac{1}{2}\right) \cdot 2^n} = \frac{(3 - 1) \cdot 2^n}{\frac{1}{2} \cdot 2^n} = \frac{2 \cdot 2^n}{\frac{1}{2} \cdot 2^n} = 2 \cdot 2 = 4$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Example 3.} Prove that $\left(a^p\right)^{q-r} \cdot \left(a^q\right)^{r-p} \cdot \left(a^r\right)^{p-q} = 1$

\textbf{Solution:}
\[
\left(a^p\right)^{q-r} \cdot \left(a^q\right)^{r-p} \cdot \left(a^r\right)^{p-q} = a^{p(q-r)} \cdot a^{q(r-p)} \cdot a^{r(p-q)}
\]
\[
= a^{pq - pr} \cdot a^{qr - pq} \cdot a^{rp - rq} = a^{pq - pr + qr - pq + rp - rq} = a^0 = 1
\]

\part{n-th Root of a}
% Defining the section for n-th Root of a
\section*{n-th Root of $a$}

We observe:
\[
5^{\frac{1}{2}} \times 5^{\frac{1}{2}} = \left(5^{\frac{1}{2}}\right)^2
\]
\[
5^{\frac{1}{2}} \times 5^{\frac{1}{2}} = 5^{\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}} = 5^{2 \times \frac{1}{2}} = 5
\]
Therefore, $\left(5^{\frac{1}{2}}\right)^2 = 5$. The square (power 2) of $5^{\frac{1}{2}} = 5$, and the square root (second root) of $5 = 5^{\frac{1}{2}}$. $5^{\frac{1}{2}}$ is written as $\sqrt{5}$.

Again, we observe:
\[
5^{\frac{1}{3}} \times 5^{\frac{1}{3}} \times 5^{\frac{1}{3}} = \left(5^{\frac{1}{3}}\right)^3
\]
\[
5^{\frac{1}{3}} \times 5^{\frac{1}{3}} \times 5^{\frac{1}{3}} = 5^{\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3}} = 5^{3 \times \frac{1}{3}} = 5
\]
The cube (power 3) of $5^{\frac{1}{3}} = 5$, and the cube root (third root) of $5 = 5^{\frac{1}{3}}$. $5^{\frac{1}{3}}$ is written as $\sqrt[3]{5}$.

In the case of the $n$th root:
\[
a^{\frac{1}{n}} \times a^{\frac{1}{n}} \times a^{\frac{1}{n}} \times \ldots \times a^{\frac{1}{n}} \quad [\text{successive multiplication of } n \, a^{\frac{1}{n}}\text{'s}] = \left(a^{\frac{1}{n}}\right)^n
\]
\[
a^{\frac{1}{n}} \times a^{\frac{1}{n}} \times a^{\frac{1}{n}} \times \ldots \times a^{\frac{1}{n}} = a^{\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{n} + \ldots + \frac{1}{n}} \quad [\text{in the exponent, sum of } n \, \frac{1}{n}\text{'s}]
\]
\[
= a^{n \times \frac{1}{n}} = a
\]
Therefore, $\left(a^{\frac{1}{n}}\right)^n = a$. The $n$th power of $a^{\frac{1}{n}}$ is $a$, and the $n$th root of $a$ is $a^{\frac{1}{n}}$. That is, the $n$th power of $a^{\frac{1}{n}}$ is $\left(a^{\frac{1}{n}}\right)^n = a$, and the $n$th root of $a$ is $(a)^{\frac{1}{n}} = a^{\frac{1}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a}$.

The $n$th root of $a$ is written as $\sqrt[n]{a}$.

\textbf{Example 4.} Simplify:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $(12)^{-\frac{1}{2}} \times \sqrt[3]{54}$
    \item $(-3)^3 \times \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $(12)^{-\frac{1}{2}} \times \sqrt[3]{54} = \frac{1}{(12)^{\frac{1}{2}}} \times (54)^{\frac{1}{3}} = \frac{1}{(2^2 \times 3)^{\frac{1}{2}}} \times (3^3 \times 2)^{\frac{1}{3}}$
    \[
    = \frac{1}{(2^2)^{\frac{1}{2}} \times 3^{\frac{1}{2}}} \times (3^3)^{\frac{1}{3}} \cdot 2^{\frac{1}{3}} = \frac{1}{2 \cdot 3^{\frac{1}{2}}} \times 3 \cdot 2^{\frac{1}{3}}
    \]
    \[
    = \frac{2^{\frac{1}{3}}}{2^1} \times \frac{3^1}{3^{\frac{1}{2}}} = \frac{3^{1-\frac{1}{2}}}{2^{1-\frac{1}{3}}} = \frac{3^{\frac{1}{2}}}{2^{\frac{2}{3}}} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt[3]{4}}
    \]
    \item $(-3)^3 \times \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 = (-3)(-3)(-3) \times \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) = -27 \times \frac{1}{4} = -\frac{27}{4}$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Work:} Simplify:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\frac{2^4 \cdot 2^2}{32}$
    \item $\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{\frac{5}{2}} \times \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{-\frac{5}{2}}$
    \item $8^{\frac{1}{4}} \div 8^{\frac{1}{3}}$
\end{enumerate}

To be noticed:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Under the condition $a > 0, a \neq 1$, if $a^x = a^y$, then $x = y$.
    \item Under the condition $a > 0, b > 0, x \neq 0$, if $a^x = b^x$, then $a = b$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Example 5.} Solve: $4^{x+1} = 32$

\textbf{Solution:}
\[
4^{x+1} = 32 \quad \text{or} \quad (2^2)^{x+1} = 32 \quad \text{or} \quad 2^{2x+2} = 2^5
\]
\[
\therefore 2x + 2 = 5 \quad \left[\text{if } a^x = a^y, x = y\right]
\]
\[
2x = 5 - 2 \quad \text{or} \quad 2x = 3
\]
\[
\therefore x = \frac{3}{2}
\]

\section*{Logarithms}

Logarithms are used to find the values of exponential expressions. Logarithm is written in brief as $\log$. Product, quotient, etc., of large numbers or quantities can easily be determined with the help of $\log$.

We know $2^3 = 8$; this mathematical statement is written in terms of $\log$ as $\log_2 8 = 3$. Conversely, if $\log_2 8 = 3$, it can be written in terms of exponents as $2^3 = 8$. That is, if $2^3 = 8$, then $\log_2 8 = 3$, and conversely, if $\log_2 8 = 3$, then $2^3 = 8$. Similarly, $2^{-3} = \frac{1}{2^3} = \frac{1}{8}$ can be written in terms of $\log$ as $\log_2 \frac{1}{8} = -3$.

If $a^x = N, (a > 0, a \neq 1)$, then $x = \log_a N$ is defined as $a$-based $\log_a N$.

\textbf{Note:} If $a > 0$, then $a^x$ is always positive whatever the values of $x$, positive or negative. So, only the logarithm of positive numbers has values that are real; the logarithm of zero or negative numbers has no real value.

$10^2 = 100$ & $\log_{10} 100 = 2$ \\
$3^{-2} = \frac{1}{9}$ & $\log_3 \frac{1}{9} = -2$ \\
$2^{\frac{1}{2}} = \sqrt{2}$ & $\log_2 \sqrt{2} = \frac{1}{2}$ \\
$2^{-\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ & $\log_2 \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = -\frac{1}{2}$ \\
$\sqrt[3]{2^4} = 2$ & $\log_2 2 = \frac{4}{3}$ \\

$e^1 = e$ & $\log_e e = 1$ \\

\subsection*{Laws of Logarithms}

Let $a > 0, a \neq 1; b > 0, b \neq 1$ and $M > 0, N > 0$.

\textbf{Formula 6 (Zero and One Log):}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\log_a 1 = 0$
    \item $\log_a a = 1$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Proof:}
We know from the formula of exponents, $a^0 = 1$. Therefore, from the definition of $\log$, we get $\log_a 1 = 0$ (proved).

Again, we know from the formula of exponents, $a^1 = a$. Therefore, from the definition of $\log$, we get $\log_a a = 1$ (proved).

\textbf{Formula 7 (Log of Products):} $\log_a (MN) = \log_a M + \log_a N$

\textbf{Proof:}
Let $\log_a M = x$, $\log_a N = y$. Therefore, $M = a^x$, $N = a^y$.
\[
MN = a^x \cdot a^y = a^{x+y}
\]
\[
\therefore \log_a (MN) = x + y
\]
\[
\log_a (MN) = \log_a M + \log_a N \quad [\text{putting the values of } x, y]
\]
Therefore, $\log_a (MN) = \log_a M + \log_a N$ (proved).

\textbf{Note:} $\log_a (MNP \ldots) = \log_a M + \log_a N + \log_a P + \ldots$

\textbf{Note:} $\log_a (M \pm N) \neq \log_a M \pm \log_a N$

\textbf{Formula 8 (Log of Quotient):} $\log_a \frac{M}{N} = \log_a M - \log_a N$

\textbf{Proof:}
Let $\log_a M = x$, $\log_a N = y$. Therefore, $M = a^x$, $N = a^y$.
\[
\frac{M}{N} = \frac{a^x}{a^y} = a^{x-y}
\]
\[
\therefore \log_a \frac{M}{N} = x - y
\]
\[
\log_a \frac{M}{N} = \log_a M - \log_a N \quad (proved)
\]

\textbf{Formula 9 (Log of Power):} $\log_a M^r = r \log_a M$

\textbf{Proof:}
Let $\log_a M = x$, therefore $M = a^x$.
\[
(M)^r = (a^x)^r \quad \text{or} \quad M^r = a^{rx}
\]
\[
\therefore \log_a M^r = rx \quad \text{or} \quad \log_a M^r = r \log_a M
\]
Therefore, $\log_a M^r = r \log_a M$ (proved).

\textbf{Note:} $\left(\log_a M\right)^r$ and $r \log_a M$ may not be equal. For example:
\[
\left(\log_2 4\right)^5 = \left(\log_2 2^2\right)^5 = 2^5 = 32, \quad 5 \log_2 4 = 5 \cdot 2 = 10 \neq 32
\]

\textbf{Formula 10 (Change of Base):} $\log_a M = \log_b M \times \log_a b$

\textbf{Proof:}
Let $\log_a M = x$, $\log_b M = y$. Therefore, $a^x = M$, $b^y = M$.
\[
a^x = b^y \quad \text{or} \quad \left(a^x\right)^{\frac{1}{y}} = \left(b^y\right)^{\frac{1}{y}} \quad \text{or} \quad b = a^{\frac{x}{y}}
\]
\[
\therefore \frac{x}{y} = \log_a b \quad \text{or} \quad x = y \log_a b
\]
\[
\log_a M = \log_b M \times \log_a b \quad (proved)
\]

\textbf{Corollary 1:} $\log_a b = \frac{1}{\log_b a}$ or $\log_b a = \frac{1}{\log_a b}$

\textbf{Proof:}
We know $\log_a M = \log_b M \times \log_a b$. Putting $M = a$, we get:
\[
\log_a a = \log_b a \times \log_a b
\]
\[
1 = \log_b a \times \log_a b
\]
\[
\therefore \log_a b = \frac{1}{\log_b a} \quad \text{or} \quad \log_b a = \frac{1}{\log_a b} \quad (proved)
\]

\textbf{Example 6.} Find the values:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\log_{10} 100$
    \item $\log_3 \frac{1}{9}$
    \item $\log_y 81$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\log_{10} 100 = \log_{10} 10^2 = 2 \log_{10} 10 = 2 \times 1 = 2 \quad [\because \log_a a = 1]$
    \item $\log_3 \left(\frac{1}{9}\right) = \log_3 \left(\frac{1}{3^2}\right) = \log_3 3^{-2} = -2 \log_3 3 = -2 \times 1 = -2 \quad [\because \log_a a = 1]$
    \item $\log_y 81 = \log_y 3^4 = 4 \log_y 3 = 4 \times 1 = 4 \quad [\because \log_a a = 1]$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Example 7.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item What is the $\log$ of $5 \sqrt{5}$ to the base of 5?
    \item If the $\log$ of 400 is 4, then what is the base of $\log$?
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\log_5 (5 \sqrt{5}) = \log_5 \left(5 \times 5^{\frac{1}{2}}\right) = \log_5 5^{\frac{3}{2}} = \frac{3}{2} \log_5 5 = \frac{3}{2} \times 1 = \frac{3}{2} \quad [\because \log_a a = 1]$
    \item Let the base be $a$. Therefore, by the question, $\log_a 400 = 4$.
    \[
    a^4 = 400 \quad \text{or} \quad a^4 = (20)^2 = \left\{(2 \sqrt{5})^2\right\}^2 = (2 \sqrt{5})^4
    \]
    \[
    \therefore a = 2 \sqrt{5} \quad [\because a^x = b^x, a^x \neq 0, a = b]
    \]
    Therefore, the base is $2 \sqrt{5}$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Example 8.} Find the value of $x$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\log_{10} x = -2$
    \item $\log_x 324 = 4$
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\log_{10} x = -2$
    \[
    x = 10^{-2} = \frac{1}{10^2} = \frac{1}{100} = 0.01
    \]
    \[
    \therefore x = 0.01
    \]
    \item $\log_x 324 = 4$
    \[
    x^4 = 324 = 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 2 \times 2 = 3^4 \times 2^2 = 3^4 \times (\sqrt{2})^4 = (3 \sqrt{2})^4
    \]
    \[
    \therefore x = 3 \sqrt{2}
    \]
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Example 9.} Prove that $3 \log_{10} 2 + \log_{10} 5 = \log_{10} 40$

\textbf{Solution:}
\[
\text{Left hand side} = 3 \log_{10} 2 + \log_{10} 5
\]
\[
= \log_{10} 2^3 + \log_{10} 5 \quad [\because \log_a M^r = r \log_a M]
\]
\[
= \log_{10} 8 + \log_{10} 5
\]
\[
= \log_{10} (8 \times 5) \quad [\because \log_a (MN) = \log_a M + \log_a N]
\]
\[
= \log_{10} 40 = \text{right hand side} \quad (proved)
\]

\textbf{Example 10.} Simplify: $\frac{\log_{10} \sqrt{27} + \log_{10} 8 - \log_{10} \sqrt{1000}}{\log_{10} 1.2}$

\textbf{Solution:}
\[
\frac{\log_{10} \sqrt{27} + \log_{10} 8 - \log_{10} \sqrt{1000}}{\log_{10} 1.2}
\]
\[
= \frac{\log_{10} 3^{\frac{3}{2}} + \log_{10} 2^3 - \log_{10} (10^3)^{\frac{1}{2}}}{\log_{10} \frac{12}{10}}
\]
\[
= \frac{\frac{3}{2} \log_{10} 3 + 3 \log_{10} 2 - \frac{3}{2} \log_{10} 10}{\log_{10} (3 \times 2^2) - \log_{10} 10}
\]
\[
= \frac{\frac{3}{2} \left(\log_{10} 3 + 2 \log_{10} 2 - 1\right)}{\log_{10} 3 + 2 \log_{10} 2 - 1} \quad [\because \log_{10} 10 = 1]
\]
\[
= \frac{3}{2}
\]

\part{Scientific or Standard Form of Numbers}
% Defining the section for Scientific or Standard Form of Numbers
\section*{Scientific or Standard Form of Numbers}

We can express very large numbers or very small numbers easily by using exponents. For example, the velocity of light:
\[
300000 \, \text{km/sec} = 300000000 \, \text{m/sec} = 3 \times 100000000 \, \text{m/sec} = 3 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/sec}
\]
Again, the radius of a hydrogen atom:
\[
0.0000000037 \, \text{cm} = \frac{37}{10000000000} \, \text{cm} = 37 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{cm}
\]
\[
= 3.7 \times 10 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{cm} = 3.7 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{cm}
\]

For convenience, very large or very small numbers are expressed in the form $a \times 10^n$ where $1 \leq a < 10$ and $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. The number of the form $a \times 10^n$ is called the scientific or standard form of a number.

\part{Logarithmic Methods}
% Defining the section for Logarithmic Methods
\section*{Logarithmic Methods}

Logarithmic systems are of two kinds:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Natural Logarithm:} Mathematician John Napier (1550–1617) of Scotland published the first book on logarithms in 1614 by taking $e$ as its base. $e$ is an irrational number, $e = 2.71828 \ldots$. Such logarithms are called Napierian logarithm or $e$-based logarithm or natural logarithm. $\log_e x$ is also written in the form $\ln x$.
    \item \textbf{Common Logarithm:} Mathematician Henry Briggs (1561–1630) of England prepared a table of logarithm (log table) in 1624 with 10 as the base. This logarithm is called Briggs logarithm or 10-based logarithm or practical logarithm. This logarithm is written as $\log_{10} x$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Note:} If there is no mention of base, in the case of algebraic expressions, $e$ is considered the base, and in the case of numbers, 10 is considered the base. In the log table, 10 is taken as the base.

\part{Characteristics of Common Log}
% Defining the section for Characteristics of Common Log
\section*{Characteristics of Common Log}

Let a number $N$ be expressed in the scientific form as $N = a \times 10^n$, where $N > 0$, $1 \leq a < 10$, and $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. Taking base 10 logarithm of both sides, we get:
\[
\log_{10} N = \log_{10} (a \times 10^n) = \log_{10} a + \log_{10} 10^n = \log_{10} a + n \log_{10} 10
\]
\[
\therefore \log_{10} N = n + \log_{10} a \quad [\because \log_{10} 10 = 1]
\]
Suppressing the base 10, we have $\log N = n + \log a$. $n$ is called the characteristic of $\log N$.

\textbf{Note:} It is evident from the table that the characteristic of a number having $m$ digits to the left of the decimal point is 1 less than the number of digits to the left of the decimal sign, and that will be positive. That is, if the mentioned number of digits is $m$, the characteristic of the logarithm will be $m - 1$.

\begin{tabular}{|p{2cm}|p{4cm}|p{2cm}|p{4cm}|p{2cm}|}
\hline
$N$ & Scientific form of $N$ & Exponent & Number of digits on the left of the decimal point & Characteristic \\
\hline
6237 & $6.237 \times 10^3$ & 3 & 4 & $4-1=3$ \\
623.7 & $6.237 \times 10^2$ & 2 & 3 & $3-1=2$ \\
62.37 & $6.237 \times 10^1$ & 1 & 2 & $2-1=1$ \\
6.237 & $6.237 \times 10^0$ & 0 & 1 & $1-1=0$ \\
0.6237 & $6.237 \times 10^{-1}$ & -1 & 0 & $0-1=-1$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}

\textbf{Note:} Now observe from the following table: If there is no integral part of a number and the number has $k$ zeros immediately after the decimal point, then the characteristic of the logarithm of the number is $-(k+1)$. If any characteristic is negative, the minus sign is not placed on the left of the characteristic; rather, a bar sign is placed over the characteristic. For example, characteristic $-3$ will be written as $\overline{3}$. Otherwise, the whole part of the log, including the mantissa, will be negative.

\begin{tabular}{|p{2cm}|p{4cm}|p{2cm}|p{4cm}|p{2cm}|}
\hline
$N$ & Scientific form of $N$ & Exponent & Number of zeros between decimal point and its next first significant digit & Characteristic \\
\hline
0.6237 & $6.237 \times 10^{-1}$ & -1 & 0 & $-(0+1)=-1=\overline{1}$ \\
0.06237 & $6.237 \times 10^{-2}$ & -2 & 1 & $-(1+1)=-2=\overline{2}$ \\
0.006237 & $6.237 \times 10^{-3}$ & -3 & 2 & $-(2+1)=-3=\overline{3}$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}

\textbf{Note:} The characteristic may be either positive or negative, but the mantissa will always be positive.

\textbf{Example 11.} Find the characteristics of the logarithm of the following numbers:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item 5570
    \item 45.70
    \item 0.4305
    \item 0.000435
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Solution:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $5570 = 5.570 \times 1000 = 5.570 \times 10^3$. Therefore, the characteristic of the logarithm of the number is 3. Otherwise, the number of digits in the number 5570 is 4. Therefore, the characteristic of the logarithm is $4 - 1 = 3$.
    \item $45.70 = 4.570 \times 10^1$. Therefore, the characteristic of the logarithm of the number is 1. Otherwise, there are 2 digits in the integral part (i.e., on the left of the decimal point) of the number. Therefore, the characteristic of the logarithm is $2 - 1 = 1$.
    \item $0.4305 = 4.305 \times 10^{-1}$. Therefore, the characteristic of the logarithm of the number is $-1 = \overline{1}$. Otherwise, there is no zero between the decimal point and its next first significant digit of the number 0.4305, i.e., there are 0 zeros. Therefore, the characteristic of the logarithm is $-(0+1) = -1 = \overline{1}$.
    \item $0.000435 = 4.35 \times 10^{-4}$. Therefore, the characteristic of the logarithm of the number is $-4 = \overline{4}$. Otherwise, there are 3 zeros between the decimal point and its next first significant digit of the number 4. Therefore, the characteristic of the logarithm is $-(3+1) = -4 = \overline{4}$.
\end{enumerate}

\part{Mantissa of Common Log}
% Defining the section for Mantissa of Common Log
\section*{Mantissa of Common Log}

The mantissa of the common logarithm of any number is a non-negative number less than 1. It is mainly an irrational number, but the value of the mantissa is determined up to a certain decimal place. The mantissa of the logarithm of a number can be found from the logarithm table. Again, it can also be found by a calculator. We shall find the mantissa of the logarithm of any number by using the second method, that is, by a calculator.

\textbf{Example 12.} Find the characteristic and the mantissa of $\log 2717$:

\textbf{Solution:}
Let us use the calculator: $\log 2717 = 3.43409$. Therefore, the characteristic of $\log 2717$ is 3, and the mantissa is 0.43409.

\textbf{Example 13.} Find the characteristic and the mantissa of $\log 43.517$:

\textbf{Solution:}
Let us use the calculator: $\log 43.517 = 1.63866$. Therefore, the characteristic of $\log 43.517$ is 1, and the mantissa is 0.63866.

\textbf{Example 14.} What are the characteristics and mantissa of $\log 0.00836$?

\textbf{Solution:}
Let us use the calculator: $\log 0.00836 = -2.07779$.
\[
-2.07779 = -3 + 0.92221 = \overline{3}.92221
\]
Therefore, the characteristic of $\log 0.00836$ is $-3 = \overline{3}$, and the mantissa is 0.92221.

\textbf{Example 15.} Find $\log_e 10$:

\textbf{Solution:}
\[
\log_e 10 = \frac{1}{\log_{10} e} = \frac{1}{\log_{10} 2.71828} = \frac{1}{0.43429} \quad (\text{using the calculator})
\]
\[
= 2.30259 \quad (\text{approx})
\]
Alternative: We use the calculator: $\ln 10 = 2.30259$.
